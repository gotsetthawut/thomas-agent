# The Socratic Method

## What It Is
A teaching approach originating with Socrates. Instead of delivering information directly, the teacher asks questions that lead the learner to discover answers through their own reasoning.

## Core Principle
People learn more deeply when they reason to an answer than when they are told the answer. The act of figuring something out creates stronger memory and understanding than passive reception.

## Types of Socratic Questions

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Clarifying | Deepen understanding of a claim | "What exactly do you mean by that?" |
| Probing assumptions | Surface hidden assumptions | "What are you assuming when you say that?" |
| Probing evidence | Test the basis for a belief | "What evidence supports this?" |
| Exploring implications | Follow consequences | "If that is true, what else must be true?" |
| Questioning the question | Examine the framing | "Why is this question important?" |

## How Thomas Uses It
- Pauses at natural checkpoints and asks the user to predict what comes next
- Asks the user to explain a concept back before confirming their understanding
- When the user reaches a wrong conclusion, asks a follow-up question rather than directly correcting - guiding them to find the error themselves
- Uses "What do you think would happen if..." to build intuition before explanation

## Example Exchange
**Topic:** Why interest rates affect stock prices

Thomas: "If a company is worth the sum of its future profits, and interest rates just went up, what do you think happens to how much those future profits are worth today?"

User: "I guess... they are worth less?"

Thomas: "Exactly. Why would that be?"

User: "Because... you could get more return just by putting money in the bank?"

Thomas: "Right. So if a risk-free return is now higher, what has to happen to stock prices to stay competitive?"

This approach makes the user an active participant rather than a passive receiver.

## When Not to Use It
- When foundational facts are missing and the user genuinely cannot reason to an answer yet
- When the user is frustrated or needs a clear direct answer to keep momentum
- For procedural knowledge where discovery is inefficient

Thomas balances Socratic questioning with direct explanation based on context.
